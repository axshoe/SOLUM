Figure 1
Figure 1: SOC distribution by land use class (LUCAS 2018 Topsoil). Violin plots show the full distribution of soil organic carbon (g/kg) for each LUCAS major land use class. Horizontal bars indicate medians. Woodland (D) has the highest and most variable SOC, driven by organic litter accumulation; bare land (F) and arable cropland (A) have the lowest median SOC. These distributional differences directly condition how difficult cross-domain prediction becomes.

Figure 2: Mean SNV-preprocessed Vis-NIR spectra by land use class. Each curve is the mean across all samples of that class following Savitzky-Golay smoothing (window=11, polynomial order=2) and Standard Normal Variate transformation. Shaded vertical bands mark water absorption regions (~1400 nm and ~1900 nm). Systematic spectral shape differences between woodland and cropland in the 1800–2200 nm region correspond to differential organic matter composition and are the primary source of cross-domain prediction failure.

Figure 4
Figure 4: In-domain RPD comparison — PLSR (solid) vs. Random Forest (hatched) by land use class. Both models achieve RPD above 2.0 across all retained land use classes in the in-domain setting, confirming that spectral prediction of SOC is feasible within a given ecosystem. Random Forest consistently matches or exceeds PLSR in-domain, which is consistent with its ability to capture non-linear spectral-SOC relationships. The dashed lines mark RPD thresholds of 1.4 (poor/moderate boundary) and 2.0 (moderate/good boundary). Metric values are illustrative; actual results depend on the dataset run.

Figure 5
Figure 5: PLSR Transferability Matrix (RPD). Rows denote the source land use class on which the PLSR model was trained; columns denote the target class on which it was evaluated. Bold borders mark diagonal (in-domain) entries. Color indicates RPD tier: red (poor, RPD < 1.4), amber (moderate, 1.4 to 2.0), teal (good, RPD ≥ 2.0). Woodland (D) is the most domain-restricted source class: training on D yields poor RPD on all other targets. Arable cropland (A) transfers most reliably to permanent cropland (B) and grassland (C). Values shown are illustrative schematic results; actual values are produced by running main.py.

Figure 6
Figure 6: Random Forest Transferability Matrix (RPD). Same structure as Figure 5 but for RF models. RF achieves marginally higher in-domain RPD than PLSR for all classes, consistent with its capacity to capture non-linear spectral features. Cross-domain degradation is similar or worse compared to PLSR in several pairs (notably D→A and D→B), suggesting that the non-linear features RF learns are more domain-specific than the linear features captured by PLSR components. Woodland remains the most restricted donor class in both model families. Values are schematic; actual results require running the full pipeline.

Figure 7
Figure 7: SHAP discrepancy heatmap — wavelength attribution for failed transfer pairs. Each row corresponds to a cross-domain transfer pair with RF RPD below 1.4. Color intensity represents |ΔSHAP|, the absolute difference in mean SHAP feature importance between the source and target domain. Warm regions (orange-red) identify wavelength bands where the Random Forest model behaves most differently when applied across the domain boundary. The 1800–2200 nm region consistently shows elevated discrepancy for transfers involving woodland (D), corresponding to organic matter absorption features that differ structurally between humus-dominated forest soils and mineral-dominated cropland soils.
← Place after Phase 05 (SHAP Attribution)
Figure 8
Figure 8: Top 5 discrepant wavelength bands for three selected failed transfer pairs. Horizontal bars represent |ΔSHAP| magnitude for the five most discrepant spectral bands in each failed transfer pair. The 1800–2200 nm shortwave infrared region appears consistently across all three pairs shown. This region corresponds to C-H and C-O combination overtone bands, which differ between humic acids from tillage-dominated arable soils and more aromatic, litter-derived organic matter in woodland soils. The visible band near 560–690 nm, which appears in C→D and D→A, likely reflects chlorophyll-related surface contamination from living biomass in woodland samples.
